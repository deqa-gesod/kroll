# BRAND.md

> The canonical brief for **Jackson & Coil** *(working name)* — a curl-specialist salon in Grünerløkka, Oslo. This is the source-of-truth document the website, identity, and salon experience are built from. Every design decision should answer to it.

---

## 0. How to use this document

- The **personas** in §4 are the ground truth. When in doubt, ask "What would Amara, Sigrid, or Daniel think?"
- **Voice rules** in §7 and the **Photography Test** in §9 are non-negotiable filters — anything that fails them is reworked or cut.
- The **moats** in §5 are the strategic spine. New ideas should reinforce them, not blur them.
- This doc evolves. Update it when reality teaches us something the brief got wrong.

## 1. The One-Liner

**Oslo's first salon built around the curl spectrum.**

From loose waves to tight coils, every pattern gets the same care, by stylists who actually trained for it.

(Sub-line, used when one is allowed: *"Grünerløkka. Founded by Vanessa Jackson."*)

---

## 2. Mission & Promise

### Mission

To be the salon that anyone with curls in Norway wishes had existed when they were sixteen — and the place the whole Nordics flies to once it does.

### Promise

Three sentences we deliver on every appointment:

1. **We see your hair before we touch it.** A free 15-minute dry consultation comes with every first visit, no obligation, no pressure.
2. **We cut you for *your* curl pattern, not against it.** Dry. Curl by curl. The way the great curl houses cut.
3. **You leave feeling at home, not negotiated with.** No upsell theatre. No "your hair is *so* thick" while the stylist looks panicked. No surprises on the bill.

### What we are *not*

- Not a regular Oslo salon that has put a curly girl on the booking page.
- Not a curly-girl-method content brand selling vibes.
- Not for one curl type or one demographic — but unapologetic about whose absence inspired it.
- Not subtle about pricing, ever.

## 3. Founder Story

> *Note: this narrative is an invented but credible scaffold. Vanessa to overwrite with her real bio before launch.*

**Vanessa Jackson** grew up in Tottenham, North London, the youngest daughter of Jamaican parents who ran a small Caribbean grocery store on Bruce Grove. Her hair was 4a and her grandmother did it every Saturday morning in the kitchen, hot-comb on the gas hob, the smell of coconut oil and burned newspaper on the windowsill. By the time she was sixteen she was doing the hair of every girl in her year for ten quid and a Lucozade.

She trained formally at Curl Bar London under Michelle Sultan, then crossed to New York for a year at the original Devachan and a season studying with Anthony Dickey at Hair Rules. She came back to London with a clear opinion: that the binary the industry insisted on — *"Black hair"* in one shop, everything else in another — was a failure of craft, not of biology.

She moved to Oslo at 28 for a Norwegian boyfriend who didn't last but a city that did. For seven years she's worked out of a borrowed chair at three different Oslo salons, building a quiet, tightly-kept client list of women and men who'd otherwise have been on flights to London or Stockholm. The salon is what happens when she stops borrowing chairs.

**Vanessa's belief, in one sentence:** *Every curl pattern is the same job done with knowledge — and knowledge is something you go and get, not something you wait for.*

**Photographed:** working. Hands in hair. Mid-cut. Mid-laugh with a client. Never staged-portrait alone in front of a backdrop. Never holding scissors like a magazine cover.

**Her voice:** direct, warm, a little dry, slightly London. She says *"yeah no it's fine"* a lot. She doesn't use the word *journey*. She doesn't use exclamation marks.

## 4. Customer Personas

Three personas, in priority order. The brand is built **for Amara first**, but it *welcomes* Sigrid and **belongs to Daniel** equally.

### 4.1 PRIMARY — Amara, 27

**Demographics**: Born in Oslo. Norwegian mother, Nigerian father. Type 4a/4b. UX designer at a fintech. Disposable income ~30k NOK/mo after rent. Lives in Tøyen, drinks at Munchies, summers in Lagos every other year.

**Her relationship to her hair**: ten years of Black-hair Instagram, knows DevaCut from Rezo from Ouidad, owns Pattern, Bread, Mielle, Camille Rose. Has flown to London for haircuts. Has cried in Oslo salon chairs. Has a permanent low-grade ache that nobody in this country sees her hair properly.

**What she needs from us**:
- Type 4 visibility on the homepage in the first 3 seconds.
- A founder she can trust on sight (Black, trained somewhere real, *not* a Norwegian who took a curly-hair course).
- Transparent pricing and no upsell theatre.
- Eventually: a community space for mixed-heritage women in Oslo. She is *isolated*, even in a city of a million people.

**What loses her instantly**: stock photos, the words *exotic / unruly / tame / whisperer*, mudcloth on a feature wall, a 90% white team with one Black face, performative AAVE in captions, an "all curl types welcome!" caveat that means "please don't think we're only Black."

**Her brand contribution**: she is the protagonist of our photography. She is whose hair is on the homepage. The entire visual system is calibrated to her.

### 4.2 SECONDARY — Sigrid, 34

**Demographics**: White Norwegian. Type 2c/3a curls. Lives in Torshov. Project manager at an architecture firm. One four-year-old. Time-poor.

**Her relationship to her hair**: was told it was "frizzy" until she found the curly-girl method on Instagram in her late twenties. Has been to four Oslo salons that promised curl expertise and didn't deliver. Pays 600 NOK for cuts that leave her crying in the car.

**What she needs from us**:
- One small signal that she belongs in the gallery — a Type 3a head visible in the portfolio, not a separate "loose curl" page.
- Confidence, not reassurance — *"we cut every curl pattern, from loose waves to tight coils,"* said calmly, once.
- Booking she can do on a phone at 22:30 after her kid is asleep, with real availability (no "we'll get back to you").
- A first-visit bundle so she's not guessing what to book.

**What she's quietly worried about**: that she's "not curly enough" for a curl specialist, and "too curly" for a regular salon. The brand's job is to dissolve that anxiety without ever naming it directly.

**Her brand contribution**: she is the *test* of inclusion. If Sigrid feels excluded, the brand has overcorrected. If Sigrid feels centered, we've lost Amara. The balance is: Amara is the face of the brand; Sigrid is in the gallery.

### 4.3 SECONDARY-PRIMARY — Daniel, 31

**Demographics**: Born in Oslo. Norwegian father, Eritrean mother. Type 4a. Software engineer at Schibsted. Wears a high-top fade with defined coils on top — the Lakeith Stanfield silhouette.

**Today's status quo**: flies to Stockholm twice a year for the actual cut (~2,000 NOK + ~1,500 NOK flights, twice = ~7,000/year), maintains the fade between trips at a Somali barber in Tøyen (~500 NOK / 3 weeks = ~8,500/year). Total ~15,500 NOK/year and two travel days. **There is no place in Oslo that does both his fade and his coils in one chair.**

**What he needs from us**:
- Both skills in one chair, in one appointment. Choreographed. Not "come back tomorrow."
- Editorial / documentary photography, not beauty / wellness photography. References: Fade Room (Toronto), Hawthorne (NYC).
- Architectural language: *sculpt, shape-up, taper, definition, line, edge.* Never *pamper*.
- Integrated everywhere. No `/men` subpage that signals afterthought.
- One-tap rebook for returning clients. He knows what he wants; don't make him fill out a form.

**The opportunity he reveals**: Black men's grooming in Oslo is a category nobody is serving competently. This is the highest-margin, lowest-competition wedge in the entire proposition. He'd pay 900–1000 NOK for the integrated cut. **We are probably underpricing the men's service relative to value.**

**Daniel's brand contribution**: he is the masculinity check. Any design move that would make him feel unwelcome is wrong. If he's in the homepage rotation, the rest follows.

### 4.4 Cross-persona truths

These are the things ALL THREE said, almost word-for-word:

| Truth | Implication |
|---|---|
| Show prices openly | Hidden prices = mistrust, full stop |
| Dry consultation, every first visit | Highest-leverage differentiator we have |
| Booking must work on a phone, at night, instantly | Cutters-grade UX or worse than nothing |
| Hero photography decides the brand in 3 seconds | Photography budget is brand budget |
| Hard-to-find products on the retail wall | Loyalty + margin + reason to revisit |
| Confidence, not reassurance | No exclamation marks. No "all welcome!" |
| Cut hair *dry* | Wet-cutting is a category-failure signal |

## 5. The Eight Moats

The strategic spine. Each is something we can deliver day one, that competitors cannot copy from a brand book.

| # | Moat | Why it can't be copied |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Cultural authenticity, not cosplay.** Diverse founding team, visible Black creative leadership. | A brand book can't manufacture this. |
| 2 | **The full curl spectrum, no kids' table.** One portfolio, all curl types, Type 4 as the home base. | Most salons are too scared to commit to Type 4 visibly. |
| 3 | **The "third category" for Black men's grooming.** Clipper-precise fades AND coil scissor-work in the same chair. | Nobody in Oslo currently does both skills under one roof. |
| 4 | **A named, owned cutting method.** *The Jackson Cut* (women) / *The Sculpt Cut* (men). | A trademark + teaching system competitors would have to build from zero. |
| 5 | **Free 15-minute first-time consultation.** Dry, no pressure, no upsell. | Most Oslo salons can't afford to give time away — we make it the brand. |
| 6 | **Cutters-grade booking on a stunning marketing site.** Real availability, weekday evenings, one-tap rebook, SMS reminders. | Norwegian competitors are 5+ years behind on booking UX. |
| 7 | **A curated retail wall solving the Norway gap.** Bread, Pattern, Adwoa, Innersense, Bouclème, Curlsmith, Cantu, As I Am, Mielle, Camille Rose, twist sponges, durags, beard picks. | We're the importer of the products our customers already buy on Amazon UK. |
| 8 | **Community as a content engine.** Monthly meetups, education evenings, kids' hair workshops, eventually a podcast in Vanessa's voice. | Earned media nobody else can replicate — because they're not the people. |

### The "premium feel at 750 NOK" formula

We can't out-spend on materials or square meters. We over-deliver on craft, design quality, and cultural specificity instead.

| Spend like a 1500 NOK salon on… | Save on… |
|---|---|
| Photography (real clients, professional, multiple shoots/year) | Interior decoration (lean Scandinavian, not theatrical) |
| Typography & web design (one great designer, not a template) | Print marketing (zero — digital first) |
| Stylist training (recurring trips to NYC/London for technique) | Big launch event (favor sustained word-of-mouth) |
| Consultation time (15 free min on first visit) | Free coffee in fancy cups (regular coffee is fine) |
| Music & sound design in the salon | Big retail markup (modest margin → people actually buy) |

## 6. Naming + Wordmark Direction

### The two finalists

**Finalist A — *Jackson & Coil*** (recommended)
- Owns the founder name and the texture word.
- *Coil* tests as more architectural and gender-neutral than *curl*.
- Strongest cultural resonance with the primary persona.
- Distinct from PATTERN Beauty.
- Risk: ties brand value to one founder's name.

**Finalist B — *The Curl Foundry*** (defensible alternative)
- Won 2 of 3 personas.
- Gender-neutral; ages well; scales independently of Vanessa.
- Risk: the primary persona called it "Brooklyn-2018, generic"; less culturally specific.

### Recommendation

**Jackson & Coil.** The primary persona's verdict outweighs the 2-of-3 craft-name vote *because the brand's fundamental moat is cultural authenticity, and a craft-name dilutes that signal.*

### The single open question for Vanessa

> *How attached are you to your name being on the door?*

If she'd rather build a brand independent of her personal exit, **The Curl Foundry** is the call. This is the only naming question that should be put to her directly before final lock.

### Names ruled out

- *Krøll & Co* — Norwegian-language pun, tested as twee with all three personas, "centers Norwegianness, not the culture the salon is serving" (Amara).
- *Mane Studio Oslo* — generic, "safari-adjacent" (Daniel), forgettable.
- Anything ending in *Studio* — overcorrects to corporate.
- *The Pattern Room* — beautiful but trademark conflict with PATTERN Beauty.

### Wordmark direction (post-name lock)

- **Custom or near-custom wordmark.** No off-the-shelf typeface alone for the logo.
- **Reads at 30 feet.** Test on a printed A3 across a room.
- **Works in all-caps and mixed case.** Salon awning vs. Instagram avatar.
- **Single owned color.** See §8.

## 7. Voice & Tone

The voice is a person — Vanessa, slightly idealized for copywriting purposes. Direct, warm, a little dry, slightly London. Confident enough not to perform confidence.

### Do

- *"We cut every curl pattern, from loose waves to tight coils."*
- *"Dry consultation, first visit, free. No obligation."*
- *"Trained at Curl Bar London and Devachan NYC."*
- *"Book on the phone in two minutes. Real availability."*
- *"Sculpt cut and taper — 60 minutes — 750 NOK."*
- *"Day-three hair, day-five hair, scarf-imprint hair. We get it."*

### Don't

- ❌ *"Embrace your curls!"* — pandering, exclamation
- ❌ *"Welcome to your hair journey"* — every D2C 2018–2024
- ❌ *"Treat yourself"* / *"Pamper"* — wellness register, alienates Daniel
- ❌ *"Curl whisperer"* — we are not horses (Amara)
- ❌ *"All hair types welcome!"* with three exclamations — reads as nervous (Sigrid)
- ❌ *"Queen / blessed / girlies / periodt"* — performative AAVE without team to back it
- ❌ *"Tribal / exotic / unruly / tame"* — never, anywhere

### Register switching

| Audience | Register |
|---|---|
| Default web copy | Direct, warm, Vanessa's voice |
| Men's services | Architectural — *sculpt, taper, definition, line, edge* |
| Education content | Informed, technical — *porosity, low-poo, dry-cutting, protein-vs-moisture, Andre Walker chart* |
| Community / meetups | Warmest register, first-name, no marketing-speak |
| Booking flow | Tightest register, every word earns its place |

### Language: Norwegian vs English

English-first copy. Norwegian where it makes sense (booking labels, location, FAQs, legal). Single click language toggle, no `/no/` URL prefix. Don't translate brand voice phrases — keep *"every curl pattern"* in English even on the Norwegian site.

## 8. Visual System

### Typography

- **Headings**: contemporary serif with humanist warmth. Direction: *GT Sectra*, *Editorial New*, *Reckless Neue*, or similar. One face, used confidently, never decoratively.
- **Body**: humanist sans. Direction: *Inter*, *GT America*, *Söhne*, or similar.
- **Utility / numbers**: a mono face with character (e.g. *Söhne Mono*, *JetBrains Mono*) for prices, times, addresses.
- **One typographic move that's ours**: a specific way we set headlines (large, generous leading, sometimes mixed-case ligature pairs) that becomes recognizable.

### Color

The single most important visual decision after photography. **One owned color.**

- **Primary** *(working direction)*: a deep terracotta-ochre — warm, textile-feeling, distinct from beauty-industry pink/sage/cream. Pantone direction: rust, burnt sienna, sun-dried clay.
- **Off-black**: not pure black. A 10% warm shift toward the primary.
- **Paper-cream**: 5% warm shift on white. Used as the dominant background. Never pure white.
- **Single accent**: a deep blue-green for booking states, success, and links. Sparingly.
- **No second brand color.** No gradient palettes. No "secondary green for the wellness section." Owned color discipline is the Glossier-pink lesson.

The terracotta-ochre is a *direction*, not a final hex. Lock it during the design phase against the photography it has to live with.

### Photography (the brand's real budget)

**Register**: editorial / documentary, not beauty / wellness.

**References**:
- *PATTERN Beauty* — joyful Black-hair editorial.
- *Fade Room (Toronto)* — Black men's documentary.
- *Hawthorne (NYC)* — masculine grooming, real light.
- *Tekla* — Scandinavian-soulful warmth.
- *Aesop* — restraint as luxury.

**Rules**:
- Real clients only. Never stock.
- Multi-context per shoot: in chair (mid-process), outside on Grünerløkka streets (finished look in daylight), candid (laughing, talking, eating during a 4-hour appointment), day-3 silk-scarf-imprint, day-5 protective style.
- Dark skin lit *properly*. Skin-aware lighting; never the standard salon backlight that turns dark skin muddy.
- Hands-in-hair photography (Vanessa working) over portrait-with-arms-crossed.
- Eye contact. Full face. Joy. Not the "back of the head" diversity-slide framing.
- 4 shoots per year minimum, varied weather and light.

### Illustration / iconography

Minimal. The one functional illustration system: **Andre Walker curl-pattern diagrams (Type 2A through 4C)** used in the booking flow and education content. Useful AND visually distinctive AND a quiet expertise signal.

### Sensory / atmospheric extensions

- **Spotify playlist** in Vanessa's voice, public, embedded on the site as a "What's playing in the salon right now" widget.
- **A signature scent in the room** — same in every appointment (consider a Diptyque or a custom blend; nothing too sweet, nothing wellness-spa).
- **A sound bed in the website hero** — subtle, music-not-foley, can be muted. Optional, only if it doesn't compromise Lighthouse scores.

## 9. The Photography Test (THE rule)

> If the hero image of the homepage doesn't pass this test, **the brand fails.**

The test, in full:

1. **Type 4 hair, dark skin, lit properly, joyful, in real-life context.** Not a wavy 2b girl with a flip.
2. **The hero rotation includes at least one Black man with a fade-and-coils cut**, integrated — not in a `/men` section.
3. **Sigrid sees one head she recognizes** somewhere in the visible portfolio without a separate "for wavy hair" tab.

If any of these fail, the photography is reshot. No exceptions. This is the most expensive rule in the document and the most load-bearing.

## 10. Service Menu + Pricing Logic

### Pricing principles

1. **Show every price on the page.** No "from 750." Hiding prices = mistrust, every persona said this.
2. **Mid-market position is a choice, not a default.** 750 NOK signals "we're priced like a normal salon, we just happen to do curls." That's the strategic play; it brings in the volume the brand needs in year one.
3. **The men's integrated cut is potentially underpriced.** Daniel's data says he'd pay 900–1000 NOK for a cut nobody else in Oslo offers. Consider 850–950 NOK for the men's *Sculpt Cut* with both clipper and scissor work. This is a numbers conversation with Vanessa.
4. **Bundles, not à la carte.** Beard trim shouldn't be 400 NOK extra on top of the cut. Bundle it. Men book maintenance packages, not line items.
5. **First visit always includes the free 15-minute consultation.** It's bundled, not a separate booking.

### Working service menu (rates to confirm with Vanessa)

| Service | Time | Price (NOK) |
|---|---|---|
| **The Jackson Cut** — dry, curl-by-curl, Type 2–4 women's hair | 75 min | 750 |
| **First Visit Bundle** — consultation + Jackson Cut + finish + first-time discount on retail | 90 min | 750 |
| **The Sculpt Cut** — fade + lineup + dry coil shaping, men's | 60 min | 850 (TBD with Vanessa — see above) |
| **Sculpt Cut + Beard** | 75 min | 950 |
| **Color, curl-safe** | from 120 min | 1,200–2,200 (consultation required) |
| **Deep Treatment** (Olaplex / protein / moisture) | 30 min | 350 add-on |
| **Free 15-min consultation** *(stand-alone)* | 15 min | 0 |
| **Children's cut (under 12)** | 30 min | 350 |

### What we won't offer (yet)

- Keratin / chemical relaxers — incompatible with our curl-affirming positioning.
- Eyelash extensions, nails, beauty add-ons — dilute the focus.
- Subscriptions / membership — founder said no for now (§15 covers phase 2).

## 11. The Owned Method

A signature cutting method gives stylists something to *teach* and clients something to *ask for*. The world-class curl houses (DevaCut, Carve & Slice, Rezo) all have one. Ours is the long-term differentiator and intellectual property.

### Working name

- **The Jackson Cut** (women / Type 2–4)
- **The Sculpt Cut** (men's, fade-integrated)

### Methodology outline (Vanessa to formalize)

1. **Assessment** — dry, on the natural pattern. Curl type, density, porosity, growth direction.
2. **Map** — identify the canopy / underlayer / nape / temple zones; the cut has different rules per zone.
3. **Cut** — curl by curl, dry, on the natural pattern. Never wet, never tension-stretched.
4. **Finish** — natural-product styling that the client will be able to recreate at home.
5. **Education** — every cut ends with the client knowing what was done and why.

### Where it lives on the site

A dedicated `/the-method` page — long-form, beautifully designed, with a 45-second video of Vanessa cutting and Andre Walker diagrams marked up with the zone map. **This is the page that converts skeptics and earns SEO.**

## 13. Booking Flow Spec

The booking flow is a brand surface, not a utility. Cutters showed this in Norway. We go further.

### The architecture

**Single-page, multi-step, mobile-first.** Lives at `/booking` and is also the action behind every CTA.

#### Step 1 — Service

- Every service shows price and duration. No "from."
- Three featured options: **First Visit**, **Jackson Cut**, **Sculpt Cut**.
- Color requires the free consultation first — surface that requirement here, don't wait until the chair.

#### Step 2 — Stylist

- Filterable: 4c specialist, color, men's fade + coils, etc.
- Default: *No preference (we'll pair you with the best fit)* — never penalized.
- **Each stylist's own calendar is browsable directly** — pick by stylist first if you want (Daniel's exact ask, fixes the "service-first then no availability" trap).
- Stylist cards show: photo, three-line bio, Instagram link, training credentials (where they trained — non-negotiable), portfolio strip.

#### Step 3 — Date & time

- **Real availability** — never "request and we'll get back to you."
- Weekday evenings included.
- "Next available" highlighted.
- Optional waitlist if a stylist is fully booked.

#### Step 4 — First-time micro-form (optional, skippable)

- Curl type (Andre Walker diagram, click-to-pick).
- Porosity (with one-line explainer per option for first-timers).
- Last chemical service.
- What you want.
- *What you're scared of.* (Amara's specific ask. This single question changes the whole dynamic.)

#### Step 5 — Confirm

- SMS reminder 24h before.
- One-tap reschedule.
- Map preview + tram directions.
- Pre-visit prep email arrives 24h before with a short Loom-style video from your stylist (Amara's specific ask — soothes first-timer anxiety).

### Returning client experience

- Logged in: "Daniel, **Sculpt Cut with Vanessa**, last visit March 14 — book the same again, next available slot." One tap. Done. (Daniel's exact ask.)
- Saved bundles: Sculpt Cut + Beard remembered as one item.
- Optional auto-suggest: *"Your last cut was 3 weeks ago — want to book the next one for around April 5?"*

### Performance budgets

- **Sigrid's 22:30 test**: a first-time booking on a phone, in bed, with a kid asleep, finishes in **under 2 minutes**.
- **Daniel's rebook test**: a returning client rebooks the same service with the same stylist in **under 60 seconds**.
- Lighthouse mobile score: 90+.

### Tech direction (recommendation, not lock)

- **Front end**: Next.js (App Router), React Server Components, deployed to Vercel.
- **CMS for the journal**: Sanity, Contentful, or Payload — whichever ages best.
- **Booking backend**: most economical near-term path is a custom UI on top of **Fresha** (Nordic salon-standard) or **Timma** (Norwegian-native). Long-term, if Fresha's API limits the UX we want, build a custom booking system on top of a calendar engine like Cal.com.
- **Booking UX requirement (non-negotiable)**: whatever backend we use, the interface is ours. We do not ship an embedded Fresha widget.
- **Auth**: passwordless email + SMS. No social login pressure.
- **i18n**: Norwegian + English. Single-click toggle, no URL prefix.

## 15. Phase-2 Roadmap

What we deliberately *don't* launch with, and when to revisit.

| Initiative | When | Why now-not-yet |
|---|---|---|
| **Membership / subscription** (e.g. 590 NOK/mo: cut + treatment + product credit) | Q4 of year 1 | Wait until we have 800+ regular clients and can underwrite the unit economics. Founder explicitly chose to launch without it; respect the focus. |

## 16. Risks & Open Questions

### Active risks

1. **Founder bottleneck.** If Vanessa is the brand AND the lead stylist AND the trainer, illness or burnout breaks everything. Mitigation: hire #2 stylist within 6 months; codify *The Jackson Cut* as a teachable method by month 12.
2. **Cultural credibility gap on the team.** Hiring almost any Oslo stylist will mean training them up on Type 4. The risk is the brand outpaces the chair experience. Mitigation: ruthless hiring; recurring training trips abroad; honesty about which stylist does what best.
3. **The 750 NOK price tag undermining the premium feel.** All three personas tested OK on it, but Amara was nervous; we may need to overdeliver on photography/site/consultation to clear the bar she set.
4. **Pandering trap.** The brand is built FOR mixed-heritage Oslo but must include Sigrid. A single overcorrection in either direction breaks it. The Photography Test is the daily check.
5. **Norway language compliance.** Norwegian customers expect Norwegian copy in core flows (booking, FAQs, pricing). English-first elsewhere is fine, but the booking flow Norwegian translation must be native-grade.

### Open questions for Vanessa

1. **Name.** *Jackson & Coil* (recommended) or *The Curl Foundry*? Tied to: how attached is she to her name being on the door?
2. **Men's pricing.** 750 NOK or 850–950 NOK for the *Sculpt Cut*? (Volume bet vs value-capture bet.)
3. **Real founder bio.** When can we replace §3?
4. **Stylist hiring plan.** Headhunting from London/Stockholm, or training Oslo talent?
5. **Color services**: launch with full color from day one, or phase in once cuts are proven?
6. **Salon address & footprint** in Grünerløkka — locked or scouting?

### Open questions for the build phase

8. **CMS choice**: Sanity vs Payload vs Contentful for the journal?
9. **Photographer**: who, when, budget?
10. **Designer**: who designs the wordmark + visual system? (This is the most important external hire after the founder herself.)
